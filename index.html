About

My journey

My Early Life

I was born in Boston, MA on September 10, 1955. That makes me 60 years old, although inside I still feel like a kid in so many ways. As far back as I can remember, I was always physically active. I loved being chased and chasing. I loved looking at the world as I hung upside down from a clothes-line. Any kind of outdoor activity was a thrill for me. I grew up in the period before organized sports. Hide and seek, touch football, hopscotch, jump rope and many other outdoor activities filled my days after school and during summer vacations. As I grew older, I became a young woman and almost forgot the good feel of exercise. However, even “all dressed up” and ready to hang out, I would grab my friends’ hands and make them run down the street with me. It was instinct.

I left my home to attend Regis College in Weston, MA. I had spent all my life in a city project. Suddenly, I was surrounded by nature and the beauty of one of the most affluent towns in the country. I immersed myself in my studies. I was a scholarship student and longed to fit in and do well. I succeeded and became accustomed to college life and made wonderful friends. I decided to major in Spanish and spent my junior year abroad in Spain, breathtaking Spain.

Yoga&Me

When I returned to Regis for my final year, I changed dorms. My new roommate had a friend who was a true “jock”. This girl, Cathy, invited me to run one day. That day changed my life. We ran for 15 minutes around the campus. It was fall. Leaves gently fell around my face. The cool breeze caressed me. My shoes went pitter-patter against the soft earth. I listened to my breath, and I connected with something deep inside me. I connected with myself.

I graduated from college and went to grad school in Monterey, California. I travelled south during that time and ended up living in Mexico City and Monterrey, Mexico for several years. Eventually, I returned to Boston. I married and had children. Throughout all these changes I kept running. I loved to race and entered many local events. I would always either win or place in the top 3. I had natural speed without any formal training. Later I joined a local running club, L Street Running Club and met many individuals who shared my love of running and racing. Some of my PRs are a 39:04 for the 10K and a 3:20 for the Boston Marathon. I went to my first yoga class when I was in my 30s. My love of yoga was not as immediate as was my attachment to running, but I kept at it. When I turned 50 I became a regular practitioner of Bikram Yoga and attended a studio in Quincy, MA. I also began to practice at Open Doors Yoga in Dorchester. Yoga was and continues to balance beautifully with my running. Both practices bring me peace and joy.

Why I Want To Teach

As I get older, I realize more and more the gazillion and one benefits that I receive from yoga. I am now 60 years old, and I still run competitively and practice yoga on a regular basis. Two years ago, my husband and I moved to New Jersey to care for his mom and sister. It was then that I decided to deepen my yoga practice by taking a teacher training course. I took a 200-hour teacher certification course with Kelly Santucci at the Younique Yoga Studio in Cedar Grove. During the training my knowledge of the history of yoga deepened and I learned step by step how to enter a pose and transition from one to another. My desire now is to teach yoga. Yoga is good for you. Regular practice can and will benefit you.

Yoga can enhance and improve athletic performance. It can help whether you play basketball or soccer, whether you swim or dance. It can prevent injury. In in any sport the repetitive use of muscles and tendons, the motion of the particular sport, will, over time, lead to injury. Ask any runner! Running results in tight hamstrings and hips, which in turn leads to knee problems. Yoga practice stretches and strengthens the hips and hamstrings. Yoga strengthens the quadriceps muscles that in turn support the knees. Many seasoned runners appear to hunch over when they run. This is because the shoulder muscles become tight and inflexible. Yoga poses open the chest and shoulders and strengthen the back muscles. Yoga is all about stretching and opening areas that are blocked. It is the perfect balance to running and many other sports. There are yoga sequences to balance any activity. These poses are designed to stretch and strengthen and when practiced properly and regularly will do their job.

Yoga&You!

If you are looking to improve your balance and strengthen your core, yoga will help you to do that. Yoga seeks to unite the mind, body and spirit. As you strengthen your core, your balance will improve. You will leave a yoga session feeling relaxed and refreshed. When you leave the day’s worries behind for a while and tune into what is going on inside you, you begin to destress. As we leave childhood behind and enter the adult world, we no longer “play” or participate in activities that naturally made us feel strong and vigorous. Many times the fatigue we feel is mental, though it may feel physical. Yoga lets you play again. You go upside down, balance on one leg, twist and turn and wake up your body once again. Our physical selves want to move. We just need to be awakened.

Yoga is being used more and more in the workplace, hospitals and schools as an effective tool to lessen depression and anxiety. There are different types of breathing that relax the mind and body when you are feeling anxious or are having trouble staying focused. Learn these techniques on the mat, and eventually you will use them throughout the day and stay calm no matter what is going on around you.

Yoga’s benefits extend to everyone. Yoga can be learned by any person. I mean EVERYONE! Try it! What do you have to lose…. maybe that headache or that pervasive sense of worry that follows you around. Maybe you’ll learn to breathe in health and calm and breathe out stress and worry. I practice yoga and it has benefitted me in all the ways I mention above. Begin your journey on the mat. You will be glad that you did.
